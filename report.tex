\begin{abstract}
The exact computation of Schatten $p$-norms for large-scale matrices poses a significant computational challenge due to the $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ complexity of the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). In this report, we discuss mathematical foundations and efficient algorithmic strategies to estimate these norms, focusing specifically on highly structured but rugged spectra arising from linear combinations of tensor powers. By unifying stochastic trace estimators with the Golub-Kahan Bidiagonalization (GKB) method, we reduce the computational memory footprint to $\mathcal{O}(N)$ while maintaining numerical stability. We validate our approach against exact values derived from SU(3) representation theory, presenting numerical results and performance scaling that highlight the absolute necessity of matrix-free Krylov architectures for high-dimensional tensor networks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Schatten $p$-norm of a matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times N}$ is defined as the $\ell_p$-norm of its singular values. Let $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots, \sigma_N$ be the singular values of $A$. The Schatten $p$-norm is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \|A\|_p = \left( \sum_{i=1}^N \sigma_i^p \right)^{1/p}.
\end{equation}
For $p=1$, this corresponds to the trace norm; and as $p \to \infty$, it converges to the spectral norm (the largest singular value). 

Evaluating $\|A\|_p$ exactly requires full knowledge of the singular spectrum, which is typically acquired via the SVD. For large dimensions, this approach is computationally prohibitive both in time and memory. Therefore, we utilize advanced numerical linear algebra techniques, specifically randomized trace estimators and Krylov subspace methods, to approximate these norms efficiently \cite{benzi2023computation, arrigo2016computation}. In particular, we focus on evaluating linear combinations of Kronecker powers, $M = c_1 A^{\otimes n} + c_2 B^{\otimes n}$, where the spectrum is highly clustered and standard decomposition solvers often fail or stagnate.

\section{Theoretical Background}

\subsection{Matrix Functions and Trace Estimation}

For $p=1$, the trace norm can be reformulated as the trace of a specific matrix function. Since the singular values of $M$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the Hermitian positive semi-definite matrix $M^\dagger M$, we can write:
\begin{equation}
    \|M\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^N \sigma_i = \mathrm{tr}\left( \sqrt{M^\dagger M} \right).
\end{equation}
Evaluating the trace of a large implicit matrix can be effectively handled using Hutchinson's stochastic trace estimator. For a symmetric or Hermitian matrix $X$, Hutchinson's estimator approximates the trace as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{tr}(X) \approx \frac{1}{s} \sum_{i=1}^s v_i^\dagger X v_i,
\end{equation}
where $v_1, \dots, v_s$ are random vectors with independent identically distributed entries satisfying $\mathbb{E}[v_i v_i^\dagger] = I$. Typically, complex Rademacher (random signs) distributions are employed. This reduces the trace computation to evaluating bilinear forms $v_i^\dagger \sqrt{M^\dagger M} v_i$ \cite{benzi2026estimation}.

\subsection{Krylov Subspace Projection}

The remaining challenge is to evaluate the bilinear form $v^\dagger \sqrt{M^\dagger M} v$ (for $p=1$) and estimate the maximum singular value (for $p=\infty$) without explicitly computing $M^\dagger M$ or its full spectrum. Standard approaches like Stochastic Lanczos Quadrature (SLQ) apply the Lanczos process directly to $M^\dagger M$. However, this explicitly squares the condition number of the operator, rapidly exacerbating floating-point roundoff errors and causing a loss of orthogonality in the Krylov basis.

To maintain numerical stability, we instead project the high-dimensional operator $M$ onto a low-dimensional subspace using bidiagonalization methods that act strictly on $M$ and $M^\dagger$, avoiding the direct formulation of $M^\dagger M$.

\section{Algorithmic Approach: Golub-Kahan Bidiagonalization (GKB)}

We unify the estimation of both the trace norm and the spectral norm under the Golub-Kahan Bidiagonalization (GKB) process. The GKB algorithm iteratively builds orthogonal bases $U_\ell \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times \ell}$ and $V_\ell \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times \ell}$ using a starting vector $v$ such that:
\begin{equation}
    M V_\ell = U_\ell B_\ell, \quad M^\dagger U_\ell = V_\ell B_\ell^\dagger + \gamma_\ell u_{\ell+1} e_\ell^T,
\end{equation}
where $B_\ell$ is a small $\ell \times \ell$ upper bidiagonal matrix. 

\paragraph{Trace Norm ($p=1$):} 
The tridiagonal matrix that would be generated by applying Lanczos to $M^\dagger M$ is algebraically equivalent to $T_\ell = B_\ell^\dagger B_\ell$. According to Gaussian quadrature rules \cite{arrigo2016computation}, we can estimate the bilinear form by evaluating the matrix square root on this dimensionally reduced matrix:
\begin{equation}
    v^\dagger \sqrt{M^\dagger M} v \approx \|v\|_2^2 \left[ \sqrt{B_\ell^\dagger B_\ell} \right]_{1,1}.
\end{equation}
Averaging this result over $s$ random Hutchinson samples yields the trace norm.

\paragraph{Spectral Norm ($p=\infty$):}
For the spectral norm, we bypass external solvers (which may fail to converge on highly clustered, complex spectra) and utilize the properties of the GKB projection itself. The largest singular value of the projected matrix $B_\ell$ provides a strict lower-bound approximation of the largest singular value of $M$. By taking the maximum singular value of $B_\ell$ observed across the $s$ random starting vectors, we obtain a highly stable and computationally inexpensive estimate of $\|M\|_\infty$.

\section{Preliminary Results}

To empirically analyze the GKB methodology, we evaluated the algorithms on linear combinations of random $3 \times 3$ complex density matrices: $M = 1.0 A^{\otimes n} - 0.5 B^{\otimes n}$. This formulation allowed us to scale the matrix dimension $N = 3^n$. 

\paragraph{Accuracy Verification:}
Traditional dense matrix constructions run out of memory (OOM) at large dimensions, making exact accuracy verification impossible. To circumvent this, we utilized a custom calculator based on SU(3) representation theory (\texttt{tensorpow}) to acquire exact, analytical ground-truth norms. This allowed us to explicitly evaluate the relative error of the GKB estimator at massive tensor powers, specifically at $n=8$ ($N=6,561$) and $n=12$ ($N=531,441$).

\paragraph{Performance Scaling:}
As shown in Figure \ref{fig:speed} and Figure \ref{fig:memory}, the $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ computational time and $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ spatial constraints of the exact dense SVD make it intractable for large systems. In our empirical runtime tests, the dense SVD was capped at $n=8$. At $n=9$ ($N=19,683$), the exact dense SVD computation required nearly 40 minutes ($\sim 2388$ seconds) for a single execution, and inevitably triggered OOM limits at $n \ge 10$. 

Conversely, the GKB method successfully bypasses these bottlenecks. Because GKB relies solely on a 3-term recurrence relation, its memory footprint remains strictly bounded at $\mathcal{O}(N)$, mathematically independent of the number of Hutchinson samples ($s$) or maximum Krylov steps ($m$).
